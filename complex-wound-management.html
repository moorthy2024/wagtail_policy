Complex Wound Management

Applicable NDIS Standards

Outcome Each participant requiring complex wound management receives appropriate support relevant and proportionate to their individual needs.

Indicators

  • Each participant is involved in the assessment and development of the plan for their complex wound management. With their consent, the participantâ€™s health status is subject to regular and timely review by an appropriately qualified health practitioner. The plan identifies how risks, incidents and emergencies will be managed, including required actions and escalation to ensure participant wellbeing.
  • Appropriate policies and procedures are in place, including a training plan for workers, that relate to the support provided to each participant requiring complex wound management.
  • All workers working with a participant requiring complex wound management have received training, relating specifically to the participantâ€™s needs that are affected by their wound management regime (for example, showering, toileting and mobility) and high intensity support skills descriptor for providing complex wound management, delivered by an appropriately qualified health practitioner or person that meets the high intensity support skills descriptor for complex wound management.

Background and Definitions

General Medical Information:

Complex wound management involves the care of wounds that are difficult to heal due to underlying conditions such as diabetes, vascular diseases, or immobility. This includes chronic wounds like pressure ulcers, venous or arterial ulcers, and diabetic foot ulcers, as well as wounds resulting from surgical procedures or trauma. Effective management requires a holistic approach, incorporating assessment, cleansing, dressing, infection control, and often, collaboration with specialists. It also involves addressing underlying causes, optimizing nutrition, and managing pain.

Definitions:

Complex Wound: A wound that is difficult to heal due to factors like its duration, depth, location, or the presence of underlying health conditions.

Chronic Wound: A wound that fails to heal within the expected timeframe, often due to underlying health issues such as poor circulation or diabetes.

Pressure Ulcer (Decubitus Ulcer): A wound caused by prolonged pressure on the skin, commonly occurring in bed-bound or wheelchair-bound individuals.

Venous Ulcer: A wound that occurs due to impaired venous return, often found on the lower legs.

Arterial Ulcer: A wound resulting from poor arterial blood flow, typically seen on the feet and toes.

Diabetic Foot Ulcer: A wound commonly found on the feet of individuals with diabetes, caused by neuropathy and poor circulation.

Surgical Wound: A wound created by surgical incision, requiring proper care to heal effectively and prevent infection.

Wound Debridement: The medical removal of dead, damaged, or infected tissue from a wound to improve healing.

Wound Exudate: Fluid that leaks out of blood vessels into a wound, containing proteins, cells, and other substances crucial to healing.

Infection Control: Practices aimed at preventing infection in a wound, including aseptic technique and the use of appropriate dressings.

Dressing Selection: Choosing the appropriate wound dressing based on the woundâ€™s characteristics, such as size, depth, and exudate level.

Moist Wound Healing: A principle in wound care that maintains an optimal moisture balance in the wound environment, enhancing healing.

Biofilm: A complex aggregation of microorganisms growing on the wound surface, often contributing to delayed healing and infection.

Nutritional Support for Wound Healing: Dietary interventions to provide adequate nutrition, essential for effective wound healing.

Pain Management in Wound Care: Strategies to alleviate pain associated with wounds, including medication and dressing choice.

Wound Assessment Tools: Instruments or scales used to evaluate wound size, depth, tissue type, and other characteristics critical to treatment planning.

Negative Pressure Wound Therapy (NPWT): A therapeutic technique using a vacuum dressing to promote healing in acute or chronic wounds and enhance healing of surgical incisions.

Wound Care Team: A multidisciplinary group of healthcare professionals involved in the care and management of complex wounds.

Policy

Commitment to providing comprehensive, individualized wound care, ensuring the highest standard of care for each participant's specific wound management needs.

Ensuring that wound care practices are in line with the latest clinical guidelines and best practices, tailored to each participant's unique health conditions.

Procedures

Participant Consent and Other Participant Considerations:

Implement a process for obtaining informed consent, ensuring participants or their guardians understand the wound care procedures and implications.

Regularly review and reconfirm consent, especially after any changes in wound care regimen or health status.

Assessment, Support/Care Plan Development and Review:

Conduct comprehensive assessments of wounds, including type, location, size, depth, and signs of infection.

Collaborate with healthcare professionals to develop a personalized wound care plan.

Regularly review and adapt the wound care plan based on changes in wound condition or overall health.

Staff Training and Requirements:

Provide extensive training in complex wound management, including wound assessment, dressing techniques, infection prevention, and pain management.

Conduct practical training sessions and competency assessments.

Facilitate ongoing education in wound care products, techniques, and evidence-based practices.

Risk Management:

Develop a detailed risk management strategy for wound care, addressing potential complications.

Implement preventive measures such as regular wound monitoring and adherence to aseptic techniques.

Reporting:

Maintain meticulous records of wound care activities, including assessments, dressing changes, and participant responses.

Establish a protocol for reporting adverse events, complications, or deviations from the care plan.

Prohibited Practices:

Define prohibited practices in wound care, such as inappropriate dressings or neglect of aseptic techniques.

Conduct regular audits and training to ensure compliance with standards.
