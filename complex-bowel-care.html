Complex Bowel Care

Purpose and Scope

This policy provides a comprehensive and detailed framework for the management of complex bowel care in participants. It aims to ensure high-quality, respectful, and individualized care, addressing both medical and personal aspects of bowel management. This policy applies to all employees and contractors of Wagtail Services who are involved in the care of participants with complex bowel needs.

Applicable NDIS Standards

Outcome Each Participant requiring complex bowel care receives appropriate support relevant (proportionate) to their individual needs.

Indicators

  • Each participant is involved in the assessment and development of the plan for their complex bowel care management. With their consent, the participantâ€™s health status is subject to regular and timely review by an appropriately qualified health practitioner. The plan identifies how risks, incidents and emergencies will be managed, including required actions and escalation to ensure participant wellbeing.
  • Appropriate policies and procedures are in place, including a training plan for workers, that relate to the support provided to each participant receiving complex bowel care.
  • All workers working with a participant requiring complex bowel care have received training, relating specifically to each participantâ€™s needs, type of complex bowel care and high intensity support skills descriptor for providing complex bowel care, delivered by an appropriately qualified health practitioner or person that meets the high intensity support skills descriptor for complex bowel care.

Background and Definitions

General Medical Information:

Complex bowel care involves comprehensive management of bowel functions for individuals with severe disabilities or health conditions that impair their ability to maintain normal bowel function. This may include individuals with spinal cord injuries, severe neurological disorders, or other conditions that affect gastrointestinal mobility and control. Complex bowel care is vital for preventing complications such as constipation, impaction, bowel obstruction, and fecal incontinence. Effective management requires a holistic approach, considering factors like diet, fluid intake, medication, and regular bowel routines.

Definitions:

Complex Bowel Care: Specialized care and interventions required for managing bowel health in individuals with disabilities or severe health conditions. It includes assessment, planning, and implementation of tailored bowel management strategies.

Constipation: A common bowel issue characterized by infrequent or difficult bowel movements. In the context of complex care, it may require interventions such as medication, dietary adjustments, or manual removal techniques.

Fecal Incontinence: The inability to control bowel movements, leading to unintentional loss of stool. Management may include dietary modifications, bowel routine scheduling, and specific continence aids.

Bowel Routine: A structured schedule of interventions (like diet, medication, and toileting) designed to maintain regular bowel movements and prevent complications.

Suppositories: Medications inserted rectally to stimulate bowel movements or administer specific medication for local action in the rectum.

Enemas: The introduction of fluid into the rectum and colon to stimulate a bowel movement, often used in cases of severe constipation or preparation for medical procedures.

Manual Evacuation: The physical extraction of stool from the rectum, used in situations of severe constipation or fecal impaction where softer methods are ineffective.

Neurogenic Bowel: Dysfunction of the bowel due to a neurological condition or spinal cord injury, affecting regular bowel function and control.

Colostomy/Ileostomy Care: Management of a surgical opening (stoma) in the abdomen for waste elimination due to various medical conditions, requiring specific care techniques and monitoring.

Digital Stimulation: A method used to induce bowel movement by gently stimulating the anal sphincter and lower rectum, often used in neurogenic bowel management.

Laxatives: Substances that soften stools or stimulate bowel movements, used in managing constipation. They come in various forms like oral, rectal, and systemic.

Bowel Obstruction: A serious condition where there is a blockage preventing the passage of intestinal contents. It may require urgent medical intervention.

Peristalsis: The wave-like muscle contractions in the intestines that propel contents forward, essential for normal bowel movement.

Fecal Impaction: A severe form of constipation where stool hardens and collects in the rectum and lower colon, often requiring manual removal or specific medical interventions.

Stool Softeners: Medications used to soften the stool consistency, making it easier to pass and reducing the risk of constipation.

Bowel Training: A program to help re-establish normal bowel movements, often involving scheduled toilet times, dietary changes, and sometimes medication.

Policy

Commitment to dignified, safe, and effective bowel care, prioritizing participant comfort and autonomy.

Ensuring that care is evidence-based, tailored to individual needs, and respects the participantâ€™s cultural and personal values.

Procedures

Participant Consent and Other Participant Considerations:

Utilize tailored communication strategies for consent, including visual aids, simplified language, or support from family/guardians where necessary.

Continuously engage with participants and their support network to ensure consent remains informed and voluntary.

Assessment, Support/Care Plan Development and Review:

Comprehensive assessment to include bowel history, current bowel function, dietary habits, fluid intake, physical activity, and medication review.

Develop a bowel care plan in collaboration with multidisciplinary teams including doctors, nurses, dietitians, and therapists.

Regularly review the care plan, especially after any health changes, hospitalizations, or at the participant's request.

Staff Training and Requirements:

Develop a structured training curriculum covering anatomy, physiology, common bowel disorders, hands-on care techniques, and interpersonal skills.

Organize regular competency assessments and scenario-based training exercises.

Facilitate opportunities for staff to attend external workshops and seminars for ongoing professional development.

Risk Management:

Implement a detailed risk assessment strategy, including participant-specific factors like skin integrity, mobility limitations, and history of bowel obstructions.

Develop emergency protocols for scenarios like severe constipation, bowel obstruction, or rectal bleeding.

Reporting:

Create a robust documentation process, including detailed logs of each care session, participant responses, and any complications.

Establish a clear chain of reporting for adverse events, ensuring timely escalation and response.

Prohibited Practices:

Detail prohibited practices, including the use of unapproved equipment, excessive force during manual evacuations, or neglecting participant feedback during care.

Regular training and audits to ensure compliance with these prohibitions.
