Subcutaneous Injections as Part of Diabetes Management

Applicable NDIS Standards

Outcome Each participant requiring subcutaneous injections receives appropriate support relevant and proportionate to their individual needs and specific subcutaneous injections and medication administered.

Indicators

  • Each participant is involved in the assessment and development of the plan for their subcutaneous injections which includes dosage measurement and calculation. With their consent, each participant’s health status is subject to regular and timely review by an appropriately qualified health practitioner. The plan identifies how risks, incidents and emergencies will be managed, including required actions and escalation to ensure participant wellbeing.
  • There are documented written or phone orders by the health practitioner prescribing the medication that trained workers may administer by subcutaneous injection.
  • Appropriate policies and procedures are in place, including a training plan for workers, that relate to the support provided to participants requiring subcutaneous injections and related medication.
  • All workers have completed training, relating specifically to the participant’s injection and medication needs and high intensity support skills descriptor for subcutaneous injections, delivered by an appropriately qualified health practitioner or person that meets the high intensity support skills descriptor for subcutaneous injections. Workers must also have a basic understanding of the participant’s related health condition.

Background and Definitions

General Medical Information:

Subcutaneous injections are a fundamental aspect of diabetes care, primarily for the administration of insulin, a hormone crucial for regulating blood glucose levels. This method involves injecting medication into the subcutaneous tissue beneath the skin, allowing for steady absorption into the bloodstream. Proper technique and consistent management are vital for maintaining blood glucose control and preventing diabetes-related complications. Understanding the types of insulin, injection techniques, and monitoring of blood glucose levels is essential for effective diabetes management.

Definitions:

Subcutaneous Injections: A method of delivering medication into the subcutaneous layer under the skin, commonly used for insulin administration in diabetes management.

Insulin Therapy: The use of insulin to manage blood glucose levels in individuals with diabetes. It includes various types of insulin, such as rapid-acting, long-acting, and premixed.

Blood Glucose Monitoring: Regular checking of blood sugar levels, crucial for managing diabetes and determining insulin dosages.

Injection Site Rotation: The practice of alternating injection sites to prevent lipodystrophy and ensure effective insulin absorption.

Lipodystrophy: A disorder of fat metabolism, occurring at insulin injection sites due to repeated use of the same area, which can affect insulin absorption.

Hypoglycemia: A condition where blood glucose levels drop too low, a potential risk of insulin therapy.

Hyperglycemia: High blood glucose levels, which can occur if diabetes is not effectively managed.

Insulin Pen: A device used for insulin therapy that resembles a writing pen, pre-filled with insulin, offering convenience and ease of use.

Glycemic Control: The management of blood glucose levels within targeted ranges.

Bolus Insulin: Fast-acting insulin taken at mealtimes to manage blood sugar spikes.

Basal Insulin: Long-acting insulin that maintains blood sugar levels steady throughout the day and night.

Insulin Pump Therapy: A method of insulin delivery using a small device that provides continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion.

Carbohydrate Counting: A meal planning method used in diabetes management, where carbohydrate intake is matched with insulin doses.

Diabetes Self-Management Education (DSME): Education and training for individuals with diabetes to enhance skills and knowledge for managing their condition, including how to administer insulin properly.

Insulin Syringe: A syringe with a fine needle used for injecting insulin; marked in insulin units.

Prefilled Syringes/Cartridges: Syringes or cartridges filled with a single dose of insulin, simplifying the process of insulin administration.

Needle Gauge: The thickness of the needle, with a higher gauge number indicating a thinner needle, often preferred for comfort in insulin injection.

Policy

Dedication to delivering specialized diabetes care through subcutaneous injections, with a strong emphasis on personalized treatment plans and participant-centered approaches.

Adherence to the latest diabetes treatment guidelines and clinical practices, ensuring safe, effective, and up-to-date care.

Procedures

Participant Consent and Other Participant Considerations:

Establish a detailed and understandable consent process for diabetes management through subcutaneous injections, explaining the benefits, risks, and alternatives.

Continuously engage with participants to reassess consent, particularly after any changes in health status, treatment plan, or insulin therapy.

Assessment, Support/Care Plan Development and Review:

Conduct thorough and regular assessments of participants' diabetes management needs, including blood glucose patterns, insulin sensitivity, lifestyle factors, and preferences.

Collaborate with a multidisciplinary team, including endocrinologists, diabetes educators, dietitians, and pharmacists, to develop comprehensive and individualized diabetes care plans.

Implement a schedule for frequent reassessment and adjustment of care plans to align with changes in the participant's condition, preferences, or advancements in diabetes management techniques.

Staff Training and Requirements:

Provide in-depth training on all aspects of diabetes care, including the pharmacology of insulin, advanced injection techniques, carbohydrate counting, and strategies for managing hypo- and hyperglycemia.

Facilitate hands-on workshops and simulation-based learning for managing diabetes emergencies and complications.

Encourage staff to participate in continuous professional development programs, including diabetes care updates and advancements in insulin delivery systems.

Risk Management:

Implement a comprehensive risk management plan for diabetes care, focusing on insulin administration errors, infection prevention at injection sites, and management of blood glucose extremes.

Introduce regular audits of diabetes care practices, including injection techniques and storage and handling of insulin and other medications.

Reporting:

Develop a detailed reporting system for diabetes care activities, recording all aspects of insulin administration, blood glucose readings, dietary intake, physical activity, and any adverse events or reactions.

Establish clear and prompt incident reporting mechanisms for any deviations from the care plan, medication errors, or significant changes in the participant's health status.

Prohibited Practices:

Clearly define prohibited practices in diabetes care, such as improper injection site rotation, reuse of needles, or administration of insulin without proper authorization or training.

Regularly review and enforce these standards through staff evaluations, audits, and training refreshers.
